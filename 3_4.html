2/22 Vocabulary you will need in this course


1. Noun: is a word that functions as the name of a speciﬁc object or set of objects, such as living creatures, places, actions, qualities, states of existence, or ideas. For example: Jhon, Paris, dog, book.
2. Adjectives are words that describe the qualities or states of being of nouns. For example: Big, cool, cheap, fast.
3. An adverb is a word that modiﬁes (describes) a verb (he sings loudly), an adjective (very tall), another adverb (ended too quickly), or even a whole sentence (Fortunately, I had brought an umbrella). Adverbs typically express manner, place, time, frequency, degree, level of certainty, etc. For example: Loudly, very, too, just.
4. A preﬁx is an aﬃx which is placed before the stem of a word. Adding it to the beginning of one word changes it into another word. For example: Dis, co, sub, ex.
5. Suﬃx: a letter or group of letters added at the end of a word to make a new word. For example: Able, ious, ness, ion.
6. A conjunction is a word that joins words, phrases, clauses or sentences. For example: And, but, while.
7. A preposition is a word or group of words used before a noun, pronoun, or noun phrase to show direction, time, place, location, spatial relationships, or to introduce an object. For example: In, on, at, by, to.

3/22 "Been to" vs. "gone to"

Present Perfect

We use it:

For something that started in the past and continues in the present:

We have lived in London for 10 years.

● Are we living in London right now?
● When did we move to London?

have/has + past participle

Been to vs. gone to

Completed visits :
They have been to Paris.

Not returned yet:
They have gone to Paris.

"Been to" vs. "Gone to"

● Been is the past participle of be.
● Gone is the past participle of go.

We can use both “been” and “gone” with the Present Perfect Tense.
● You can use “been” to describe completed visits.
● You can use “gone” to describe that you went to a place and you haven’t returned.

For example:
He's been to Australia three times.
She's gone to Paris. She will be back next week.

Time expressions

For : Refers to periods of time.
Recently : Not long ago.
Since : From a particular time in the past until now.
Lately : In recent past.

Recently and Lately

Recently is usually defined as 'not long ago'.
Lately is usually defined as 'recently or in the recent past'.

In the end, you can use them interchangeably in most of the cases.

Lately is also used to describe a repetitive event of the past. For example:
I have been studying a lot lately.

Recently, on the other hand, is used to show the occurrence of something in the near past, but it isn’t necessarily something repetitive. For example: I went to the gym recently.

10/22 Using "you" in an impersonal form

Using "you" in an impersonal form

Not always but usually, the personal pronoun “you” does not refer to any person or any group of people in particular. Instead, it refers to “all the people”.

For international flights, you must arrive at least 3 hours before.
For international flights, people must arrive at least 3 hours before.

Write 5 things that people can’t do in your country using the impersonal pronoun “You”.

Personal

You shouldn’t send those messages.

General

You have to show your passport at the airport.
People have to show the passport at the airport.
You have to be crazy to do that.
People have to be crazy to do that.

“As” vs. “Like”

Let’s practice: Complete the sentences using “as” or “like”.

1. She works ______ a teacher.
2. We can go somewhere _____ the mountains or the beach.
3. He likes hotdogs as much _____ I do.
4. They didn’t go to the party ______ they didn’t have any money.
5. She called me _____ I was driving.
6. Jack looks exactly _______ his father.
7. I work at a bank _____ my sister.
8. ___ I said before, this is a great idea.

As + Noun Like + Noun
As your brother, I’ll help you with that. Like your brother, I’ll help you with that.
“In the role of” I am your brother. “Similar to” I’m not your brother.
Common ways to use “as”

● To talk about jobs = He works as a teacher.
● To express similarity = She likes pizzas as I do.
● To replace “because” = I stayed at home as it was raining.
● To replace “when” = I saw her as I was going to the store.
● To compare = You work hard, as me.

Common ways to use “like”

● To give examples = I’ve been to many countries, like Russia, France and Italy.
● To express similarity using “look”, “sound” and “smell” = It tastes like chicken.
● To compare = You work hard, like me.

13/22 Comparisons with adverbs


There are adverbs that are also adjectives

He runs fast = “Fast” is an adverb.
He is a fast runner = “Fast” is an adjective.

1. One-syllable adverbs = fast, hard,high
Add -er to the adverb
* He runs faster than you.

2. Two-syllable adverbs = quickly,often, recently
Add more before the adverb
* He did the exercise more quickly than me.

3. Irregular adverbs = badly, well, little
* I did badly on the test.

1. Cars go fast but planes go faster.
2. Trains go more slowly thanplanes.
3. I won because I played better.
4. You need to drive more carefully.

20/22 Reporting past orders

“Tell” and “ask” to report orders and requests

Direct speech Indirect speech
The doctor said: “You need to exercise more”. The doctor told me to exercise more.
Can you please be quiet? He asked me to be quiet.

Other verbs used to report orders and requests are: command, order, warn, advise, invite, beg, teach, & forbid.
